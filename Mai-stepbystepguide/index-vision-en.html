I.3 · understanding the map · 3 min

First of all: what even is
a point in embedding space?

This embedding space is like a vast word cloud — the language model's entry map, just drawn in radically simplified form (the model itself is the many layers above it). Every word is turned into numbers and given a fixed place during training. "King" and "Queen" were used similarly across billions of texts — so they sit side by side. The same goes for Dog · Puppy · Cat · Mouse.

Things that have nothing to do with each other sit far apart: Filing cabinet.

Important: a single word is a point. A whole argument or a longer text is no longer a point — it is a cloud of many points, an entire region in space.

This geometry is the AI's real tool — not "understanding", but proximity in space.

The AI has no picture in its head. No feeling. No memory.
It sees only patterns and distances.

Your four examples on the same map.

Every "before" lands in the lower left — everything that smells of Excel lists and meeting marathons. Every "after" in the upper right — everything that smells of automation and clarity.

Four arrows. All pointing in the same direction.

Your fifth point — and the calculated end point.

The AI takes your before-sentence, averages the direction from the four other arrows — and places your after-point along that line. That is all. No magic, no guessing — geometry.

Note: tonight we use Claude, ChatGPT or Gemini — whichever you prefer. Results vary slightly between models. That is normal.

Common mistake

If your examples are inconsistent or too few (under 3), the AI simply guesses — it copies stylistic clichés instead of learning your real standard. Three clear before/after pairs beat ten half-baked ones every time.

If you want to go deeper

Strictly speaking, the AI does not average vectors — it uses attention between tokens. The image of parallel arrows is a 2D idealisation of the phenomenon. And: the fixed word proximity (king/queen) and the learning from your examples during the conversation are related, but not the same computation — the first sits fixed in space, the second happens freshly in context. In practice nothing changes. Three clear before/after pairs remain the most powerful tool.

Aside And what happens mathematically when the AI gives you an answer to your prompt? Expand

As soon as you enter a prompt, the model continues the text word by word. Each time, it picks the next word that best fits the text so far.

Simplified, the model thinks: "I have the first eight words. With what probability is word 9 now 'the', or 'a', or 'suddenly'?" The most probable one is chosen. Then word 9 is there — and the question starts again for word 10. And again. And again.

Incidentally: this is also why longer texts often become more error-prone. When generating word 500, the model still has to remember word 1 — and the longer the context, the harder that gets.

Does this hold for reasoning models too? Classic models are like an expert you ask a question and who starts speaking immediately, without drawing breath — at the first word they don't yet know how the sentence will end.

Reasoning models are the same expert — but when you ask the question, they first pause for 30 seconds, work through different solution paths, discard the bad ones, correct their own mistakes — and then give you the considered answer.

If you want to go deeper

The AI doesn't actually type words but sub-word tokens from a vocabulary of 50,000–100,000 units. A word like "delay" can consist of two or three tokens. In practice nothing changes. But this is exactly why long texts become more error-prone — every token step can drift.

II.4.1 · If you want to go deeper — where does the model live?

Cowork — the app — sits on your computer. The model itself, the language cloud, does not sit there: it runs in the cloud at the provider (Anthropic, Google, OpenAI …). When the agent reads your file, it briefly sends the necessary excerpt up to the language cloud, gets an answer back, sends the next question up if needed — and so on. The app is local, the thinking happens in the cloud.

What does this mean for confidential matters? In a browser chat (claude.com, ChatGPT) everything you upload passes through someone else's servers. With the Cowork agent the file stays on your computer — but the conversation history (what you type, what the agent sends along as context) is still stored on servers. Even in the paid Pro version, where your history is not used to train the next language model, the conversation logs still sit on their servers. And under the US Cloud Act, any US company can access all the data it holds — even if it physically sits on EU servers. If the agent reads a sensitive file and talks about it, that excerpt ends up in the log. Cowork is better, not private. Anonymise highly confidential material (client files, patient records, M&A drafts) before letting it read them — no real names, no case numbers. Assume the conversation ends up in the log.

and the principle behind it

The steering wheel is called — context.

That's exactly what you just did: you gave the agent context about yourself — like onboarding a new colleague. "Look in my folder my-profile", "read my emails", "here's my CRM — understand who my clients are." The better it knows you and how you work, the better it works.

But: with every sentence, every file, you turn a steering wheel. Too little — it guesses. Too much — it loses the thread (the cloud overflows — more on that in a moment). Just right: clear context, no noise.

At session start the aide wakes up and fills itself with everything in the claude.md — who it is, the project, your rules. As you work, it keeps filling up: the files you point it at, your back and forth. This store is called its context window — picture it as a cloud over the desk.

At some point the cloud is too full: it grows sluggish and starts talking nonsense — because for every answer it has to read through the whole overstuffed cloud again. Then the pro trick: close the session, open a new one, on the same folder. The old cloud rains out, the desk is clean. It reads the claude.md again, has the memory/ folder ready — so it instantly knows again who it is and where the project stands. All the waffle is gone, it's razor-sharp again.

★ Several agents in parallel On the same folder you can also open several sessions at once — essentially run several identical agents in parallel. They all share the same claude.md and the same memory/ folder, so they all know who you are — they just work on different things at the same time.

### My strategic context:

• The decision that makes the difference for me: Answering the question: What kind of AI can — structurally, legally and philosophically — be built and successfully scaled ONLY by Europeans?

### Benchmark examples (the before-after logic of excellent systems):

Food safety & regulation —
Before (sluggish standard system): A country operates on the after-the-fact principle. Harm has to be proven scientifically and legally before a product can be banned. Consumers struggle with opaque ingredients, while companies offload risk onto the public.
After (benchmark lever): A system establishes the strict precautionary principle (EU rule). Substances must prove they are safe *before* they reach the market. Trust is so enormous that these certified products (e.g. European baby food) become a priceless status symbol and grey-market premium product in unregulated markets like the US.

Pensions & demographics —
Before (sluggish standard system): Pure pay-as-you-go systems collapse under demographic change. They get stuck in a permanent tax-subsidy spiral, place a massive burden on the working population and at the same time produce widespread old-age poverty.
After (benchmark lever): A resilient system lever (the Swiss three-pillar model) couples an income-independent pension with no contribution ceiling to real, privately managed capital formation. The wealthy pay in a lot but receive a capped maximum rate. The system withstands ageing and secures prosperity in a socially fair way.

Healthcare & incentives —
Before (sluggish standard system): Fragmented systems incentivise illness through the "fee-for-service" model. More operations, more medication and more hospital days mean more revenue for hospitals. Keeping the patient healthy is financially unattractive.
After (benchmark lever): Vertical integration (Kaiser Permanente logic). Insurer and provider are the same entity. The system makes money when people stay *healthy*. It invests heavily in digital prevention and care and proves that profitability and excellent patient care go hand in hand when the incentives are right.
