Computational Governance

Date TBA · Berlin · By Invitation Only

Hosted by Damian Boeselager & Franziska Wittleder

Why this conversation matters

Can AI help societies govern themselves more effectively?

Most discussions about AI focus on risks, regulation, and disruption. An equally important question is often overlooked.

Advances in machine learning now allow us to simulate complex economic and social systems, test policy interventions before implementation, and explore new models of democratic decision-making.

This emerging field — often called Computational Governance — raises profound questions about the future of democracy, public institutions, and collective intelligence.

Discussion Themes

Four questions at the threshold of AI and democracy.

AI as a Policy Laboratory

What if governments could test policies in realistic simulations before deploying them in society?

Recent research has demonstrated how AI systems can model economic behavior and design policy interventions that improve outcomes across competing objectives such as prosperity and equality.

Could governments eventually use AI to stress-test public policy before it affects citizens?

The Challenge of Reward Engineering

AI can optimize almost anything. The real question is: what should it optimize for?

Before AI can help solve societal challenges, humans must first define what success means.

How should democratic societies translate values such as freedom, equality, sustainability, and prosperity into systems that can guide AI-driven decision-making? Who gets to decide?

Beyond Human Bias

AI often reflects historical biases found in data. But what if AI could also help identify and correct those biases?

Can computational systems help create fairer institutions than the ones humans have historically built? What role should regulation play?

Europe's Opportunity

Europe has a unique opportunity to shape democratic approaches to AI governance.

Can Europe become a global leader in combining technological innovation with democratic legitimacy?

Format

Build, dine, debate.

Part I · 2 Hours

Build

Hands-on exploration of AI agents and governance infrastructure. Participants work in small groups to prototype systems such as:

  • Policy research agents
  • Legislative analysis agents
  • Citizen feedback systems
  • Deliberation support tools
  • Public consultation infrastructure
  • Collective intelligence workflows

No prior AI expertise required. The objective is not technical perfection — but first-hand experience with the capabilities and limitations of modern AI systems.

Dinner Break · Informal Conversation

Part II · 2 Hours

Debate

An intensive salon discussion exploring questions such as:

Can AI improve democracy?

Can computational systems help societies make better collective decisions?

What should AI optimize for — and who defines societal objectives?

How do we encode democratic values into intelligent systems?

Can governance become computational? Could future governments simulate policies before implementing them?

Could AI become a policy-testing environment?

Europe's Opportunity

Can Europe lead the development of democratic AI infrastructure?
