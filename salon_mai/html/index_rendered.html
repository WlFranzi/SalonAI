Day 2See the Power of Context

Much like a new hire, AI can only be as good as the context it has about you and the work you need to get done.

Today we’re going to do a quick, five-minute exercise to see the difference in output when you don’t give context compared to when you do.

Open claude.ai in two different browser tabs. In each tab, select Claude Opus from the model selector.

Tab 1

I'm a at . I want to spend more of my time on the work that actually matters and less on low-impact work. What should I cut, change, or do differently? Don't ask me any follow-up questions.

2.

Don’t read the output yet. Switch to Tab 2.

3.

Take a screenshot of a week-long view of your calendar (on Mac, press ⌃⇧⌘ 4 to copy to your clipboard; on Windows, press Windows key + Shift + S). I'm a at . I want to spend more of my time on the work that actually matters and less on low-impact work. What should I cut, change, or do differently? Don't ask me any follow-up questions. Here's how my time actually looked last week: .

4.

Switch back to Tab 1. Read both responses side by side.

Tab 1 gave you a generic productivity listicle. Tab 2 looked at your actual week and named real things you could change. The lesson isn’t just “give AI more stuff” — it’s that figuring out what context will make AI most useful is itself a skill. Any time you’re working with AI, ask: what does it need to know to do this well, and how do I get it that information efficiently?

Key Takeaway

Day 4How to Catch AI Mistakes

AI can be particularly helpful when you are doing work that falls outside your area of expertise. However, this is also when you’re least equipped to spot a mistake or lapse in judgment. Today’s exercise teaches you Hilary's "Swiss Cheese Check" — where you stack validation steps to reduce the chances of an error slipping through.

1.

Open claude.ai in your browser and start a new conversation. Select Claude Opus from the model selector.

Paste this prompt and hit Enter

I'm a at . I have a direct report who's been underperforming for about 3 months - missing deadlines, disengaged in meetings, and the quality of their work has dropped noticeably. I've had two informal conversations but nothing has changed. I'm not sure whether to put them on a formal performance improvement plan or try a different approach. I've never done a PIP before. What do you recommend? Don't ask me any follow-up questions.

2.

Read the recommendation.

3.

Now run it through four checks, one at a time. Paste each prompt below and hit Enter before moving to the next.

Check 1: Confidence

What's the probability that this recommendation is correct? What would make you more or less confident in it?

Check 2: Context

Under what circumstances would this recommendation be wrong?

Check 3: Expert

If a world-class expert in this subject reviewed this recommendation, what would they add or change?

Check 4: Verification

How should I verify this? If I were to fact-check your recommendation on my own, what could I do?

No single check catches everything, but stacking them reduces the risk. You can run these four checks on any AI output, especially when the stakes are high or the domain isn’t yours.

You’re done for the day. Tomorrow: teaching AI your standards.

Key Takeaway

No single validation catches everything. Stack imperfect checks together and the odds of a real error slipping through drop dramatically.

Day 5Teach It Your Standards

Sometimes, you don’t want the work to reflect generic best practices; you want it to reflect your specific taste or judgment. The simplest way to teach that to the AI is to give it examples of work that you believe are good, then ask it to articulate what those examples have in common.

1.

Open claude.ai in two different browser tabs. In each tab, select Claude Opus from the model selector.

Tab 1

Paste this prompt and hit Enter

Write an update from the CEO to the company about a quarter where we missed our revenue target by 8%. Don't ask me any follow-up questions. This is a hypothetical example.

2.

Don’t read the output yet. Switch to Tab 2.

Tab 2

Paste this prompt and hit Enter

Here are three examples of executive communication that I think are excellent. What do they have in common? Articulate the specific principles that make all three work. Be precise. Example 1 - CEO quarterly update: Q3 revenue came in 12% above target. Two things drove it: the enterprise tier launched in July and pulled in $2.1M in net-new ARR, and the onboarding redesign cut time-to-value from 14 days to 6, which showed up directly in retention. Our biggest risk going into Q4 is churn in the mid-market segment, which ticked up last month. I'll walk through the full plan Thursday, but the short version: we're shifting two engineers from growth to retention for the next 6 weeks. Come with questions. Example 2 - VP announcing a team reorg: Starting next Monday, the infrastructure and platform teams are merging into one group under Priya. Here's why: we've been splitting work across two teams that share the same codebase, the same on-call rotation, and most of the same stakeholders. That means duplicated standups, competing priorities, and too many handoffs on work that should be owned by one team. No one is being let go. Priya and I will do 1:1s with everyone on both teams this week. If you have concerns, bring them - I'd rather hear them now than find out in three months that something isn't working. Example 3 - Director sharing a postmortem: On Tuesday we had a 47-minute outage that affected roughly 12% of our customers. Root cause: a config change that bypassed our normal review process. We've already shipped a fix that prevents that specific failure, but the real issue is that the review process was easy to skip. This week we're adding a hard gate. I want to be clear: the person who made the change followed a process that we allowed to exist. The system was the problem, not the individual.

3.

Read the principles it extracted.

4.

Paste this follow-up and hit Enter.

Follow up with this

Now write an update from the CEO to the company about a quarter where we missed our revenue target by 8%. Apply those principles. Don't ask me any follow-up questions. This is a hypothetical example.

5.

Switch back to Tab 1. Read both CEO updates side by side.

The first one is what AI produces by default. The second one was written to a specific standard that you defined through examples. Once your standards are explicit, you can reuse them anywhere.

You’re done for the day. Tomorrow: learning to troubleshoot.

Key Takeaway

Give AI examples of great work and it writes to that standard.

Day 6Learn to Troubleshoot

This week you’ve been chatting with Claude in the browser. That has real limits: you can’t control what it remembers about you, and it can’t take actions on your computer. To go further, we need to install the Claude desktop app, which can read and write files and actually do work.

Installing new software is also the kind of task where something unexpected tends to come up — an error, an unfamiliar screen, a prompt to install something else. That’s today’s real lesson: when something weird happens, take a screenshot and ask AI to walk you through it. This one skill will unstick you more than anything else in this program.

1.

Go to claude.ai/download in your browser and click the download button for Mac.

2.

Open the downloaded file and drag Claude into your Applications folder, just like installing any other Mac app.

3.

Open Claude: press ⌘Space to open Spotlight, type Claude, and press Enter.

4.

Click Get started, then log in with your Claude account — the same one you’ve been using at claude.ai.

Once you’re logged in, you’ll land on a screen called Cowork. Cowork is great for individual tasks, but we want to go further and build a system that levels up your whole job. For that, we need Claude Code. Don’t let the name fool you — it’s just as useful for people who’ve never touched code.

5.

Click the Code button in the top left corner of the sidebar (next to Cowork).

This is the skill. Not knowing what every screen means — but knowing that a screenshot and a clear ask will almost always get you unstuck.

Key Takeaway

When you aren’t sure what to do next, take a screenshot and ask AI to walk you through it. This is the most durable skill in the program.
